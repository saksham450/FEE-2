Detailed Review: No Longer Human by Osamu Dazai

Trigger Warning: This review contains discussions of depression, mental illness, and suicide.

No Longer Human, the last complete work by Japanese author Osamu Dazai before his tragic suicide in 1948, presents a profound and haunting exploration of human despair. As a semi-autobiographical novel, it mirrors much of Dazai's own life through the character of Oba Yozo, who grapples with depression, alienation, and addiction.

Themes and Characterization

The narrative delves deeply into the alienation and mental struggles that plagued Dazai, depicted through Yozo's life experiences. From his failures in love to his brief involvement with the Communist Party, the story captures the fragmented tenderness and pervasive sorrow of his existence. Set in post-World War II Japan, the book poignantly portrays the societal context and the lack of discourse on mental illness during that era.

One of the most striking aspects of No Longer Human is its portrayal of childhood depression. Yozo's sense of "otherness" and his inability to connect with those around him are depicted with visceral clarity. Despite the time and cultural differences, these feelings resonate universally, highlighting the timeless nature of Dazai's insights.

Writing Style and Translation

Dazai's writing is characterized by its raw honesty and lack of melodrama, even when describing Yozo's profound feelings of inhumanity. The translator, Donald Keene, manages to convey the content and mood effectively, though some nuances of the original Japanese form are inevitably lost. This brings an interesting perspective on the beauty of translation and how different editions might offer varied experiences.

Interplay with Society and Work Culture

Interspersed with Yozo's personal struggles are examinations of society and work culture. These reflections add depth to the narrative, making it both interesting and moving. Yozo's fleeting moments of hope, followed by inevitable plunges back into depression, are depicted with a stark realism that many contemporary depictions of mental illness strive to achieve.

Artistic Adaptations

The novel's impact is further enhanced by its various adaptations, such as Junji Ito's manga version. Ito's visceral illustrations provide a different lens through which to experience Yozo's torment, blending Dazai's original Japanese with the English translation and graphic art.

Literary Merits and Warnings

While No Longer Human excels in its portrayal of mental illness and alienation, it is important to note that the book is character-driven with a loosely structured plot. It may not appeal to readers seeking action and adventure. The climax is more of an emotional and psychological realization rather than a traditional narrative peak.

Potential readers should approach the book with caution. Its graphic depiction of depression and the thought processes associated with it can be triggering. This is not a cheerful or easy read, and it demands a level of preparedness from the reader.

Conclusion

In conclusion, No Longer Human serves as a powerful mirror reflecting the human condition, particularly the struggles with mental illness. Though it was written almost 75 years ago, its semi-autobiographical nature makes it resonate with modern depictions of depression and anxiety. The novel's portrayal of inhumanity is deeply human, offering a reflection that is both profound and, at times, unsettling.

This book is recommended for those who are ready to confront the raw realities of mental illness and face the reflections it presents. No Longer Human is a poignant and enduring piece of literature that continues to offer valuable insights into the human psyche.
